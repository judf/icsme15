ABSTRACT

Android applications are increasingly used in mobile devices. In July 2013 the Google Play store reached 50 billion app downloads since its creation. Android apps are similar to regular Java desktop applications, but with important differences. In particular, in Android apps it is commonplace to use late-binding mechanisms that allow components to interact. These late-binding mechanisms can end their execution signaling an exception. However, there is still little empirical knowledge about the impact that the adoption of these mechanisms has on software robustness. This paper address this gap by presenting an empirical study aimed at understanding the relationship between changes in Android programs and their robustness. In particular, we evaluated how the use of late-binding mechanisms and exceptional code were related to exception handling faults. We applied a change impact analysis and a control flow analysis in 112 versions of 16 Android programs. The results showed that: (i) most of the problems hindering software robustness in those programs are caused by changes in the normal code, (ii) many potential faults were introduced even when improving exception handling in Android applications, and (iii) faults are often facilitated by the massive use of unchecked exceptions. Moreover, we present a series of change scenarios that negatively impact program robustness.
